Anishenabe Three Fires Confederacy

Cate Dombrowski

History of Disposession

As American colonizers spread across the land known as the United States, they encountered the indigenous people who were already living there. Colonizers believed that it was their right and duty to expand across this landscape, a belief referred to as Manifest Destiny. Engagements between the two groups during this time were often incredibly violent, “strong military forces were frequently utilized against American Indians” and the United States government paid scalp bounties to encourage colonizers to kill American Indians (Ziibiwing 3). Indigenous people were “coerced...into signing treaties” and forced into small reservations which made them reliant on the US government, due to the loss of their historical hunting and fishing grounds (Ziibiwing 4). During this time, white Americans had two differing ideas for how to resolve what they referred to as the “Indian problem”. Assimilationists believed that forcing indigenous people to live within the bounds of “mainstream white society including farming, Christianization, the English language, concepts such as democracy and private property, and eventually U S citizenship” would be the best way to keep indigenous people from hindering the expansion of colonial America (National Park Service). Their counterparts, exterminationists, believed indigenous people could not assimilate and that their cultural practices were uncivilized, thus violent extermination was necessary to continue to expand (National Park Service). President Ulysses S Grant was decisively assimilationist and began to implement policies federally to assimilate indigenous people to white culture. Grant also implemented a “peace policy” which dictated management of reservations to “various Christian dominations” (National Parks Service). Many political leaders supported assimilation over extermination because they found a cultural genocide to be more a more economically feasible way to commit genocide.Around this time, an army officer named Richard Pratt opened the first Indian boarding school. Pratt took indigenous prisoners of war, cut them off from their ancestral homeland by sending them to a boarding school where their hair was cut, they wore uniforms, and were forced to learn English and practice Christianity.

Pratt was the man who coined the ubiquitous phrase “kill the Indian, Save the man”. This was a violent and traumatizing experience for the prisoners subjected to it. However, many did learn English and colonial cultural practices which led to the experiment being seen as a success by the American government. Pratt took his experiment to Congress and “requested funding for the similar education of all American Indians” (Ziibiwing 5). This boarding school model spread rapidly across the country and “by 1900 most American Indian children were being taken from their families”, severed from their culture and homeland, and forced to assimilate with American culture (Ziibiwing 5). The US government used boarding schools to dispossess indigenous children of their culture and coercive treaties dispossessed indigenous tribes of their ancestral lands. This legacy of dual dispossession exists across the United States and can be used as a framework to understand the level of violence that indigenous people have faced.

Cultural Dispossesssion: Mt. Pleasant Indian Industrial Boarding School

This project focuses on the violence committed against the Anishinaabe Three Fires Confederacy, in present day Michigan. Michigan was not precluded from the spread of Indian Boarding Schools. One of the largest in the area was the Mount Pleasant Indian Industrial Boarding School which operated from 1893 to 1934. The school was created to fulfill part of the 1855 treaty with the Anishinabek. Many treaties included promises of formal education in return for indigenous lands, including much of the ancestral homeland of the Anishenabe Three Fires Confederacy. However, this was not a positive exchange, Anishenabek people lost their land and indigenous children were sent to Mount Pleasant Indian Industrial Boarding School which utilized Richard Pratt's model of cultural genocide. Anishinabek children were forcibly removed from their families and traumatized from the moment they arrived at the school. Children were “disinfected by having alcohol, kerosene, or DDT… poured on them” (Ziibiwing 10). DDT is one of the most toxic pesticides known to man. All cultural and spiritual practices were cut off including cutting long hair, removal of personal belongings “such as medicine pouches, beadwork, family photographs, etc.” and removing traditional clothing and replacing them with uniforms (Ziibiwing 10).
